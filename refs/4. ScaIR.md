## **1. Introduction** 

As an important part of network control, traditional packet routing decides the forwarding interface of each packet based on certain simple criteria, such as to transfer data along paths with the fewest hops or lowest bandwidth-related cost in Open Shortest Path First (OSPF) and Intermediate System-to-Intermediate System (IS-IS) [1]. However, it may cause serious congestion in hotspot areas [2]. As shown in Fig. 1(a), the orange shortest path obtained by OSPF protocol takes more time than the blue path obtained through Intelligent Routing (IR) aiming at minimal delivery time. Due to the ability to generalize complex problems, Machine Learning (ML) based IR approaches can take Quality of Service (QoS) performances like the delivery time, which is 

determined by a variety of factors and cannot be calculated directly by mathematical model, as the routing criterion [3,4]. However, in the trend of Internet of Everything (IoE), data collection and decision deployment are time-consuming, and the scalability of routing is faced with huge challenges. 

Deep learning methods such as Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) are used to train on a large number of datasets and the routing table is constructed on the control plane based on the globally collected network information [5–7]. However, the supervised learning methods described above are more suitable for classifying such problems with relatively few and discrete labels. More studies use deep Reinforcement Learning (RL) to solve routing problems, which include both centralized and distributed methods. In a centralized method, such as in Software-Defined Networking (SDN), routing decisions are made by a centralized intelligent controller, but bring a lot of constraints, such as poor scalability, high latency, huge interaction and computing overhead [8–10]. Multi-agent methods, generative adversarial network and federated learning are used to improve the fast reconvergence of RL routes in dynamic network scenarios [11, 12]. And federation learning further uses sparse graph theory to prune and eliminate irrelevant federation nodes to reduce training and communication costs [13]. But they still rely on a centralized control center, or require the exchange of a large number of neural network parameters. 

While in a distributed method, decisions are made by each distributed Intelligent Router (IRr) independently [14]. Multiple routers always need to deploy IR algorithms cooperatively in a large-scale distributed network. Through independent decisions of distributed routers, the delay and communication cost can be greatly reduced. For the scalability and autonomy of multi-domain networks, Chen et al. adopt a distributed deployment strategy for federated service chaining [15]. However, it is difficult to fully understand end-to-end network characteristics for local nodes in Distributed IR (DIR), the global optimality of DIR falls short when compared to that of centralized IR [16–18]. An example is shown in Fig. 1(b). The path represented by blue lines is the routing decision based on global information, which can deliver packets in less time. In contrast, the path represented by orange lines is a joint decision made by all distributed routers on the path based on information within their local scope, which takes longer to deliver because node 0 chooses node 1 with less load between node 1 and node 5. 

Under the premise of low interaction overhead, global information acquisition is very difficult for the optimization of DIR. The emergence of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for network modeling and optimization provides a new idea [19,20]. Neural networks can compress and extract a large amount of network information into the form of feature vectors with significantly fewer bits, thus greatly reducing the interaction overhead. Compared with other models, such as RNN and CNN, GNN is built based on the graphic structure itself, which enables it to carry out more accurate feature mining and modeling for graphic systems such as social networks, molecular structures, knowledge graphs, and communication networks [21]. However, if each IRr 

builds an entire GNN based on the whole topology, it also has huge computational pressure and poor robustness to the dynamic topology. 

In this paper, we adopt the distributed routing decision architecture to mitigate the limitations of centralization and enhance the autonomy of the network itself, and propose a novel Scalable Intelligent Routing based on Distributed Graph Reinforcement Learning, called ScaIR. The main contributions and novelties of our paper lie in these points: 

- (1) We design a fully distributed GNN model to minimize communication cost and computational time while improving the global optimization of DIR. GNN is used to mine global network characteristics for improving global optimization under distributed architecture. Especially, GNN here is also distributed. Each IRr has an independent sub-GNN, which is based on the topology relationship among all nodes within the one-hop range of the IRr. Instead of entire network information and model parameters, each sub-GNN only iteratively interacts with its neighbors and computes a highly compressed Feature Vector (FV) representing the current network state, which greatly saves the computing and communication cost. In the **Evaluation** , we compare it with the method that each IRr builds an entire GNN based on the whole topology, verifying the low computational and communication costs of sub-GNN. 

- (2) A routing algorithm and system based on distributed GNN and multi-agent Graph Reinforcement Learning (GRL) are further designed. Each router is an independent agent based on local GRL, and the connectivity between multiple agents is determined by the network topology. The output of the sub-GNN acts as the input to RL. Each IRr only completes its own decision-making and optimization. The optimization goal is to minimize the delivery time. The forwarding time between IRrs and the estimated subsequent time required by the next IRr are taken as the inputs of RL. It has good performance even in the case of local congestion. The system maintains low complexity and inference time, even after introducing an additional GNN model, which is critical for practical deployment. We compared the inference time of our system with PER-D3QN and DQRC, demonstrating that our method has shorter inference time and lower computational complexity. 

- (3) We carried out extensive simulation experiments under multiple real network topologies of different scales, even including a large network with 100 nodes and 115 edges. Simulation results show that ScaIR reduces forwarding time by more than 25% and achieves faster convergence. It can better adapt to congested, dynamic or unknown environments. Compared to other methods, it significantly reduces communication cost and computational time and has better scalability. In addition, by changing the FV length of sub-GNNs, it is verified that GNN does play a key role in ScaIR. 

The rest of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the background and motivation for this study. Section 3 introduces the problem statement and system model. Section 4 describes the designed fully distributed GNN model and multi-agent GRL algorithm of ScaIR. The simulations and resulting performance are analyzed in Section 5. Finally, Section 6 concludes this paper.

We take any one of these IRrs as an example, describe the details of ScaIR algorithm, including distributed GNN, DRL mechanism, and procedure of the learning algorithm. 

## _4.1. Distributed GNN_ 

The main intuition behind the GNN in our approach is to fully mine and model global characteristics of the whole network by GNN so that to bridge the performance gap between distributed local decision making and global decision making. However, if each IRr builds an entire GNN based on the whole topology, it also has huge computational pressure and poor robustness to the dynamic topology. In this subsection, we introduce the distributed GNN designed in our algorithm in details with the help of mathematical models, corresponding to _𝑁𝐺𝑠𝑢𝑏_ in Fig. 3, as one of the inputs to _𝑁𝑄_ . 

As shown in Fig. 4, a schematic diagram of distributed GNN taking IRr4 as an example is given based on Fig. 2. The GNN model in Fig. 2 is consistent with the entire network topology, while the sub-GNN in Fig. 4 is based on the topology of one-hop neighbors. To highlight the above difference, a larger topology is introduced in Fig. 4, which includes five additional nodes from _𝑁_ 5 to _𝑁_ 9. In conventional GNN usage, a GNN model should be built based on all 9 nodes, whereas the sub-GNN in this paper is built based on the neighbor relations within one hop of _𝑁_ 4. The local topology of each IRr can be mapped to a subgraph. It is worth noting that the scope of the local topology in question varies with the number of hops defined. In this paper, the one-hop range is mainly considered, then the local topology between all nodes in the one-hop range of each IRr is mapped as a subgraph. Each node in the subgraph corresponds to a IRr in the network and each edge corresponds to a communication link between IRrs. 

Each node _𝑛_ initially has a feature representation _𝑖𝑛_ . The concept behind GNNs is called message passing, where each node iteratively interacts with its neighbors and updates its feature representation to obtain the final target value _𝑜𝑛_ . Each node _𝑛_ maps its initial feature _𝑖𝑛_ to a hidden representation and updates its hidden representation based on the hidden representation of its one-hop neighbors in the iterative process until a stable equilibrium state is reached. Finally, the hidden representation is transformed into a target value which is an abstract representation of the global characteristics of the network, and used for subsequent routing decision-making. ## _4.2. DRL formulation_ 

We introduce the mechanism of DRL in this subsection. In multiagent RL, all nodes are equivalent and have the same neural network structure. Each node can be regarded as an independent agent, which learns and makes decisions based on the observation of local network state and communication with its neighbors. Therefore, we take any node _𝑛_ in the network as an example and describe the definition of each element in RL [37]. 

**State Space** : We assume that the packet currently being processed is packet _𝑝_ . The state space of agent _𝑛_ is defined as _𝑆𝑛_ ∶{ _𝑑𝑝, 𝑂𝑛, 𝐾𝑛_ }, where _𝑑𝑝_ is the destination of packet _𝑝_ , _𝑂𝑛_ is the information observed by the node itself, and _𝐾𝑛_ is the message sent by neighbors. More specifically, _𝑂𝑛_ is the information related to the forwarding time to each neighbor node including queue status and action history, and _𝐾𝑛_ is the information possessed by agent _𝑛_ related to global network characteristics, that is the output _𝑜𝑛_ of _𝑁𝐺𝑠𝑢𝑏_ . With the change of network topology and traffic, the state of nodes also changes from time to time. 

**Action Space** : In the packet routing problem, the action space of agent _𝑛_ is mainly determined by the set of neighbors. We define the action space of agent _𝑛_ as _𝐴𝑛_ ∶ _𝑊𝑛_ , where _𝑊𝑛_ is the set of neighbor nodes of node _𝑛_ . Accordingly, the action space of each agent has the same size as its set of neighbor nodes. For each packet to be processed, agent _𝑛_ first obtains the current state _𝑆𝑛_ , then selects the optimal action from the action space _𝐴𝑛_ , and forwards the packet to the corresponding neighbor node. 

**Cost** : We design the cost to guide the choice of action toward our target to minimize the average delivery time. The cost of forwarding packet _𝑝_ is defined as the sum of queueing time and transmission delay, _𝑐𝑡_ = _𝑞_ + _ℎ_ , where _𝑞_ corresponds to the queuing time in agent _𝑛_ and _ℎ_ is the transmission delay to the next hop. 

_𝑁𝑄_ is built with fully connected layers and the output of _𝑁𝐺𝑠𝑢𝑏_ serves as its input. Overall, the input of _𝑁𝑄_ mainly consists of four parts. 

- (1) Current Destination: The destination node is used to determine the action space of the current packet. 

- (2) Queue Length: The queue length for each interface is used to characterize the congestion degree of each neighbor node. 

- (3) Feature Vector: The feature vector of its own is used to capture and characterize global network features, which is updated by _𝑁𝐺𝑠𝑢𝑏_ based on feature vectors of neighbors periodically. 

- (4) Action History: The executed actions for the past _𝑘_ packets sent out just before the current packet. 

In addition, the above information needs to be one-hot encoded before it can be entered into _𝑁𝑄_ . For example, if the destination node of the current packet is node 2, the one-hot encoded result for the first part of the input information is [0010000]. The length of the one-hot vector is usually fixed at an upper limit to ensure compatibility. 

In order to avoid local optimality, we also introduce a greedy strategy. At each decision moment _𝑡_ , if a packet _𝑝_ arrives at the queue head of agent _𝑛_ , agent _𝑛_ first gets the state information _𝑆𝑛_ , including the queue information _𝑂𝑛_ of its own and the feature vector obtained by communication with neighbors represented by _𝐾𝑛_ . The packet _𝑝_ may be delivered by the application layer of agent _𝑛_ or forwarded by neighbors. The number of episodes and the number of packets per episode are set as 100 and 50 respectively. When _𝐷𝑟_ is less than 0.4, the average time of ScaIR is longer than OSPF, and when _𝐷𝑟_ is greater than 0.4, the average time of OSPF exceeds that of ScaIR in case of traffic congestion. 

Backpressure is a dynamic routing method which routes based on real-time queue lengths of neighbor nodes, where the length of the queue indicates the level of congestion [16]. OSPF Traffic Engineering (TE) metric extensions introduce several Type-Length-Value (TLV) structures for collecting real-time network status, including network load and transmission delay [44]. This enables OSPF to no longer rely solely on static information for routing. To more thoroughly analyze the fundamental reasons why ScaIR outperforms non-ML methods in congested scenarios, we expand the comparison to include dynamic decision-making methods based on real-time information, such as Backpressure and OSPF-TE. The metrics and online information for these two methods are progressive. Here, OSPF-TE uses the real-time roundtrip time between all sources and destinations as the criterion for route selection. The only difference between this method and ScaIR is that it does not use ML. Comparing it with ScaIR can fully demonstrate the advantages of ML. The comparison results are shown in Table 4. Backpressure only considers local congestion, and cannot deal with global congestion, which in turn limits its performance. The round-trip 

time on which OSPF-TE is based requires a certain amount of time to relay back, which leads to a latency in its routing planning and prevents it from responding promptly to congestion situations. Traditional time prediction methods also fail to capture nonlinear relationships. Compared to the extended method of OSPF, ML grants ScaIR more accurate predictive capabilities, allowing for timely responses to changes in network status. Particularly, deep learning methods are able to make predictions based on a broader range of state information, whereas traditional methods would incur substantial storage and retrieval costs to achieve similar performance. 

ML approaches excel in managing large-scale, complex networks by learning from data to optimize various QoS, leading to more efficient network management and resource allocation. In contrast, traditional routing strategies like **OSPF or Backpressure** , even with enhancements, are often limited to specific indicators and lack flexibility for dynamic network conditions. Current enhanced routing protocols, such as **enhanced OSPF, IS-IS and Segment Routing** , still rely on the Dijkstra algorithm at their core. These methods require manually set rules and parameters, which limit their adaptability to new scenarios. Furthermore, **Heuristic and Exhaustive search methods** , although capable of finding solutions in some instances, often suffer from slow convergence rates, making them unsuitable for real-time or large-scale systems. Overall, ML provides effective tools for handling dynamic, large network environments, essential for the future development of intelligent network technologies and services. 

## _5.2.4. Collaboration with TCP congestion control_ 

As we all know, the transport protocol is usually responsible for congestion control in existing public networks. Clearly defining the relationship between the algorithm in our paper and existing TCP/IP protocols is indeed very important for the engineering application of the algorithm. Therefore, we specifically explain the differences and connections between ScaIR and TCP protocol as follows. 

- TCP and ScaIR are designed from two different perspectives: reliability and low latency. TCP controls the flow rate of data to avoid network congestion, thus ensuring packet reachability and network reliability, while ScaIR bypasses the congested area in the case of network congestion by optimizing the choice of paths to reduce the transmission delay. TCP can be understood as the basis for routing algorithms. ScaIR is a further optimization of TCP protocol. 

- Although certain variants of TCP, such as TCP Vegas, optimize congestion control by incorporating Round-Trip Time (RTT) to reduce latency, TCP remains an end-to-end control method that sacrifices some throughput and cannot fully utilize transmission resources to maximize the reduction of service latency. 

- In order to facilitate the management of different protocol layers, the current ScaIR and TCP protocol are independent of each other. In addition, cross-layer information interaction may introduce additional interlayer interaction delay. 

- If we can overcome the overhead and management issues associated with cross-layer interactions, and consider further optimization with interlayer messages, we initially envision the following types of information that can be invoked from the TCP layer: 

   - **Network congestion status:** The routing algorithm can adjust routes based on the current network congestion, and this information can be obtained through TCP’s congestion control mechanisms. Parameters like TCP’s receive window and congestion window can reflect the current level of congestion in the link. 

   - **Packet loss and retransmission status:** TCP protocol ensures the reliability of data transmission through acknowledgment mechanisms and timeout retransmissions. Routing algorithms can use this information to judge the quality of certain routing paths and adjust routing decisions to avoid potential problematic paths.

In the context of the Internet of Everything (IoE), there is an exponential increase in network scale and traffic. Consequently, designing scalable routing methods that can accommodate rapidly increasing network scale and traffic is a significant current challenge. The distributed mode has become an inevitable choice. However, the distributed approach only uses local information and hence may struggle to find the optimal solution. In this paper, we propose a novel Scalable Intelligent Routing based on Distributed Graph Reinforcement Learning, called ScaIR. Due to the scalability requirement, ScaIR adopts multi-agent based distributed routing mode instead of centralized routing mode. Each router is an agent, and all intelligent routers share the routing pressure of the entire network together. Distributed GNN is used to mine global network characteristics as input to RL for improving global optimization. Each router has an independent sub-GNN based on the adjacency relationship between its one-hop neighbors. Instead of entire network information and model parameters, each sub-GNN only iteratively interacts with its neighbors and computes a highly compressed FV representing the current network state, which greatly saves the computing and communication cost. Our trade-off is mainly to abandon global information and optimal solutions of the centralized mode, and choose a distributed mode to distribute the computation and communication pressure of a single control node. Under this premise, we explore the new approach to find the optimal solution, that is, distributed GNN. We carried out extensive simulation experiments under multiple real network topologies of different scales, even including a large network with 100 nodes and 115 edges. Simulation results show that ScaIR has lower forwarding delay and can better adapt to congested, dynamic or unknown environments. It can also complete efficient routing in largescale networks and has good scalability. In addition, by changing the FV length of sub-GNNs, it is verified that GNN does play a key role in ScaIR. 

Based on the comprehensive analysis presented in this paper, the following conclusions have been drawn further: 

- With the diversification and complexity of network scale, service types and traffic characteristics, the QoS of network is difficult to be calculated directly with a linear mathematical model. Machine learning has nonlinear modeling and abstraction capabilities. Compared with traditional static and linear routing based on mathematical model, Intelligent routing based on ML is the inevitable trend of complex networks in the future. 

- Compared with label-based supervised learning, reinforcement learning is more suitable for routing decision problems. 

- GNN is a powerful tool for network feature mining and modeling, and can be further widely used in routing, load balancing, network security and other fields. With the increase of network scale and traffic complexity, the required length of feature vector also increases. 

- In a wide range of distributed network control and decision problems, distributed GNN can be used as a powerful means of network situational awareness, which can significantly reduce computing and interaction overhead.
